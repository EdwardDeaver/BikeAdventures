(WRITTEN DEC 2025)
A month ago I got into [Unbound 200](https://www.unboundgravel.com/events-200/) – a brutally fast gravel race in Emporia, Kansas.

Also, earlier this year I injured my lower back, leading to an MRI and finding mildly bulging/compacted disks. This coupled with finding I'm a good 2 inches shorter than what my drivers license says it's no wonder when I  bike packed this year in the ADK and did the IRR both times my lower back was screaming in pain after 70 miles on my LeMond; a bike that I think is for someone 4 inches taller than me. My bikes are too big. They’re fine for a cross race, or a 20 mile road ride but not long rides. So I decided to get a new bike for this, one that fits, one that can bike pack and race.

I made a list of requirements:

* It needs to fit so like a ~53/54  - medium or in Canyon’s world small (always check the geo chart).
* Light weight - this is sub 23 lbs.
* Mounting points on the fork (water is very hard to come by).
* Simple (AXS or friction) - more on this later, but I wanted something that would survive multiple crashes and I really dislike dealing with indexing.
* Wide range, gearing high enough to not spin out on the flats and downhills, and low enough to granny gear it (sub 0.9 ratio)
* Wide tire clearance min – 40mm Is recommended for Unbound and IRR so that.
* Tubeless tires
* I didn’t want aluminum for this or steel. You can feel everything on aluminum and steel as much as a I love it rusts and flexes a lot when loaded up – feeling the rear end bend is not confidence inspiring.
* Budget: sub 2500 (this is going to shift - you'll never hit cheaper than oem with a diy build)

I approached this from a spec approach not a brand approach. (Edit: from the future: I'm more sentimental than I thought)

Modern bikes are thru-axle – my wheels on the LeMond (Bontrager Paradigms) came as  thru-axle but were converted to qr – they can be converted back and have the right hub spacing. (eventually will upgrade the wheelset – maybe a Chinese new years sale).

I initially wanted AXS because you can mix and match parts, I wanted a 2x setup to have road gearing high end and low end, but this was not compatible with the frame I wanted and most gravel bikes are 1x. This would also be very expensive – so I’d need to find used parts – if I crashed it’d be a very expensive repair.

So I made an excel sheet with parts, links, status (new/used), weight, and price.

Gearing: SRAM XD goes down to a 10t vs Shimano’s 11t, Ethirteen makes a 9-50 cassette with a 40t chainring that’s a 0.8 climbing gear ratio and 4.4 ratio on the high end. Other ratios: with a 10-44 12s cassete that’s a 0.9-4.0, and with a Shimano 11-36 – 1.1-3.63.

I don’t want to have multiple shifter systems, I’ll stick with my Gevenalle frictions.

While it would be nice to have hydraulic brakes I can probably get away with cable discs for now, stopping was never the issue, traction was.

Similarly on the deraloior side I like how Madrone is approachi g repairability and customizability. Also comes in purple.

BB – ceramic would be cool but expensive and I’m not sure it matters on the bb, I also like Wolftooths products. I wanted their BB in purple.

At this point you might be thinking – wow it sounds basically like a mountain bike. Yes, this is basically a modern version of a 90s mtb but with dropbars and disc brakes.

The three bespoke parts:

Madrone derailleur, Gevenelle shifters,  and WolfTooth BB. I like small companies especially ones that are on the outskirts of industries and breaking molds, also they all answer my weird questions. All of them are into the right-to-repair ethos especially WolfTooth and they're all American companies, and WolfTooth is mostly made in Minneapolis (i <3 Minneapolis).

A special note on the Madrone derailleur, 12 speed is getting more and more proprietary with deep vendor lock-in with SRAM and Shimano cable pulls differing from their 11 speeds. Madrone is kicking that issue with different cams, so you can have one derailleur, swap the cams and swap the shifters boom, you're on a new drivetrain. They also make repairable parts, and replacement parts for AXS - the repair ethos is in the company's blood. With any niche company claiming interoperability you have to bet that they'll exist in a few years. With Ratio coming out, the Growtech equal control shifters, I think we're starting to see a trend of people wanting the high end parts but not wanting lock-in. But maybe that's just an algorithmic bubble, even if it is, so be it. 

These 3 parts I could have gone for more mainstream versions and cut probably 400 dollars off the build but I'll put my money where my mouth is with these, and they look cool ;) .
